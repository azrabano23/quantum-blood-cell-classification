ABSTRACT
--------
We present a feasibility study comparing quantum machine learning (QML)
approaches against classical baselines for detecting Acute Myeloid Leukemia
(AML) from blood cell microscopy images. Our goal is not to show that quantum
methods outperform classical ones—they do not, under current hardware
constraints—but to establish whether they can produce meaningfully comparable
results, which would justify further investigation as quantum hardware matures.

We evaluate two methods: Equilibrium Propagation (EP), an energy-based
learning algorithm that trains entirely without backpropagation, and a 4-qubit
Variational Quantum Circuit (VQC) implemented on a classical simulator (IBM
Qiskit). Both are trained on engineered features extracted from the publicly
available AML-Cytomorphology dataset (18,365 expert-annotated images), using
small balanced subsets of 50–250 samples per class—realistic for scenarios
where expert annotation is costly.

All experiments were performed on a laptop computer using Qiskit simulation;
no actual quantum hardware was used. Under these conditions, EP achieves
[X.X ± Y.Y]% accuracy (mean ± std across N seeds), and VQC achieves
[X.X ± Y.Y]%, compared to 98.4% for a classical CNN. The performance gap is
substantial in absolute terms but much narrower when accounting for the
severity of the constraints—reduced image resolution (64×64 pixels), a 20-
dimensional feature representation, and simulation overhead. VQC in particular
shows stable performance across dataset sizes (50 to 250 samples/class),
suggesting better data efficiency than CNN at small sample counts.

The deeper contribution is methodological: EP's compatibility with analog
neuromorphic hardware and physical relaxation systems, and the VQC's
compatibility with near-term NISQ devices, suggest these approaches deserve
continued study even before they match classical accuracy.


I. INTRODUCTION
---------------
Manual review of peripheral blood smears is time-consuming and subject to
inter-observer variability. Deep learning has largely solved the accuracy
problem—convolutional networks now exceed human-level performance on
leukocyte classification [1],[2],[3]—but these classical models depend on
backpropagation and require substantial compute. For resource-constrained
settings, or for researchers exploring whether emerging hardware platforms can
contribute to medical imaging, this raises a practical question: how well can
quantum or quantum-inspired algorithms do on a real clinical dataset, running
on modest hardware right now?

We focus on blood smear analysis specifically because it is less invasive than
bone marrow aspiration, and because the AML-Cytomorphology dataset [1]
provides a well-annotated benchmark at scale.

Quantum machine learning (QML) has advanced alongside the development of
Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum (NISQ) devices. Two families of methods are
relevant here. Variational Quantum Circuits (VQCs) encode classical data into
quantum states via parameterized circuits, then optimize circuit parameters
using a classical outer loop—sidestepping the need for purely quantum
optimization. Equilibrium Propagation (EP) [8] takes a different route: it is
an energy-based learning algorithm, compatible with Hopfield-like network
architectures, that updates weights by comparing two equilibrium states
(free phase versus a lightly guided "nudged" phase). It never computes
gradients of a loss function, and requires no backpropagation.

This distinction matters for quantum systems. Backpropagation requires
measuring intermediate activations, which in a quantum circuit collapses the
quantum state—destroying the superposition and entanglement properties that
make quantum computing interesting in the first place. EP avoids this entirely.
Its weight updates are local: each connection's update depends only on the
states of the two neurons it connects, before and after nudging. This makes
EP naturally compatible with analog neuromorphic hardware and physical
relaxation-based computation—platforms where gradient propagation is infeasible
but energy minimization is cheap.

This paper evaluates both methods on AML detection from blood cell images, using
a laptop and Qiskit simulation. Our aim is explicit: not to claim quantum
advantage, but to characterize how close these methods get to classical
performance under realistic constraints, and to identify where future work
should focus.

Contributions:
  • A reproducible EP pipeline for AML detection, trained without
    backpropagation, using only image-derived features.
  • A 4-qubit VQC classifier (ZZFeatureMap + RealAmplitudes ansatz, COBYLA
    optimizer) benchmarked at multiple dataset scales.
  • Aggregate statistics (mean, standard deviation, standard error of the mean)
    across [N] random seeds—reporting honest performance ranges rather than
    single-seed point estimates.
  • Discussion of dataset imbalance, the value of early comparison studies,
    and EP's relevance to neuromorphic hardware.

II. RELATED WORK
----------------
Quantum methods for medical image classification remain at an early stage.
Prior work includes quantum support vector machines for MRI tumor
classification [9], exploratory VQC studies on synthetic histopathology data,
and quantum approaches to leukemia detection [10]. A key limitation across
this literature is scale: most studies use toy datasets (MNIST, synthetic
data) rather than large clinical collections with expert annotations. Recent
surveys [6] identify the central challenges as (i) moving beyond benchmark
datasets, (ii) handling real-world noise and class imbalance, and
(iii) demonstrating any practical advantage over classical methods.

This work is directly positioned within these open challenges. We use a
clinical dataset of 18,365 expert-annotated images rather than synthetic data,
and we compare quantum-inspired (EP) and pure quantum (VQC) methods under
identical experimental conditions—same images, same features, same train/test
splits. Our 4-qubit, depth-12 VQC is designed to fit within current NISQ
device constraints, and we report results across multiple seeds rather than
cherry-picking a single run.

We do not claim this work resolves the challenge of demonstrating quantum
advantage. It is a stepping stone: an honest benchmark that shows how these
methods perform on real data, with the computational resources most
researchers actually have access to.


III. METHODS
------------

III-A. Dataset and Experimental Protocol

We use the publicly available AML-Cytomorphology dataset [1], available at
https://doi.org/10.7937/tcia.2019.36f5o9ld. The dataset contains 18,365
single-cell blood smear images from 200 patients (100 AML, 100 healthy),
acquired at Munich University Hospital using May-Grünwald-Giemsa staining
at 100× oil immersion. Images are labeled by board-certified hematologists
following WHO diagnostic criteria [11]. The classification task is binary:
AML-positive cells versus healthy leukocytes.

An important note on class distribution: in the original dataset, healthy
cells substantially outnumber AML cells. This reflects a clinical reality—
most people are healthy—but it creates class imbalance that can inflate
accuracy metrics and bias models toward the majority class. To avoid this
confound, all experiments use balanced subsets with equal numbers of samples
per class. This also makes the task harder in one sense (the model cannot
exploit base rates), while ensuring that accuracy measures genuine
discrimination rather than class-frequency exploitation.

All methods operate on images downsampled to 64×64 pixels. This resolution
reduction is deliberate: it reflects the memory and computation budget of
running 4-qubit quantum circuits on a laptop, and it creates a controlled,
comparable test bed. The CNN baseline runs at the same resolution, ensuring
fair comparison.

Experimental subsets: we construct balanced datasets at 50, 100, 200, and
250 samples per class (100–500 total). All experiments use stratified 80/20
train-test splits. Results are averaged over [N] random seeds, with standard
deviation and standard error of the mean reported.

III-B. Feature Extraction

EP and VQC both operate on a 20-dimensional feature vector computed directly
from each image—not from any pre-existing annotations or metadata in the
dataset. All features are derived purely from pixel values of the 64×64
grayscale image.

The extraction procedure is: (1) load the image, (2) convert to grayscale
by averaging RGB channels, (3) resize to 64×64 with anti-aliasing,
(4) normalize pixel values to [0, 1]. Then extract five categories of
features as described in Table I.

TABLE I: 20-dimensional feature vector. All features computed from 64×64
grayscale pixel data. No external annotations used.

Category          Feature                 Description
---------         -------                 -----------
Intensity (5)     Mean intensity          Average pixel value across image
                  Std intensity           Standard deviation of pixel values
                  Median intensity        Median pixel value
                  25th percentile         Lower quartile pixel intensity
                  75th percentile         Upper quartile pixel intensity

GLCM Texture (5)  Contrast                Local intensity variation (GLCM)
                  Dissimilarity           Mean absolute difference of pixel pairs
                  Homogeneity             Diagonal closeness of GLCM
                  Energy                  Sum of squared GLCM elements
                  Correlation             Linear dependency of gray levels

Morphology (4)    Normalized area         Fraction of pixels in above-mean mask
                  Eccentricity            Elongation of the largest connected region
                  Solidity                Ratio of region area to convex hull area
                  Extent                  Ratio of region area to bounding box area

Edge Metrics (3)  Mean edge response      Mean Sobel filter magnitude
                  Std edge response       Standard deviation of Sobel magnitudes
                  Max edge response       Peak Sobel filter magnitude

FFT Frequency (3) Mean magnitude          Mean of FFT magnitude spectrum
                  Std magnitude           Std of FFT magnitude spectrum
                  Peak magnitude          Maximum value in FFT magnitude spectrum

For morphological features, we first threshold the normalized image at its
mean value to create a binary mask, then identify the largest connected
region (using scikit-image regionprops). The resulting area, eccentricity,
solidity, and extent describe the shape and coverage of the dominant cellular
structure in the image. These are entirely image-derived quantities—no cell
boundary annotations, no manual measurements.

For VQC, the 20-dimensional feature vector is further reduced to 4 dimensions
by PCA (retaining ~95% of variance) and rescaled to [0, 2π] to match the
domain of quantum rotation gates.

III-C. Variational Quantum Circuit Classifier

A VQC combines a quantum feature map with a trainable ansatz circuit,
optimized using a classical outer loop. This hybrid structure is what allows
VQCs to run on near-term hardware: the quantum circuit executes a fixed
computation parameterized by classical variables, and an outer classical
optimizer updates those variables based on the measurement outcomes. No
gradient is computed inside the quantum circuit; no intermediate state is
measured during a single forward pass.

We use 4 qubits, a ZZFeatureMap (reps=2, full entanglement) for data
encoding, and a RealAmplitudes ansatz (reps=2, 12 trainable parameters).
The circuit depth is 12, which fits within current NISQ coherence budgets
(IBM Quantum systems currently support ~100 qubits with ~100 μs coherence).

Training: each iteration, the full circuit is executed with current parameter
values, the expectation value ⟨Z₀⟩ of the first qubit is measured, and MSE
loss between ⟨Z₀⟩ and the ±1 target label is computed classically. The
COBYLA optimizer (gradient-free) then proposes new parameters. This repeats
for 200 iterations. Classification: ⟨Z₀⟩ > 0 → AML, otherwise Healthy.

All VQC experiments use classical statevector simulation via Qiskit 0.39.0.
Simulation gives upper-bound performance (no shot noise, no gate errors, no
decoherence), so actual hardware results would be somewhat lower.

III-D. Equilibrium Propagation

EP [8] trains a continuous Hopfield-like network by comparing two equilibrium
states. In the free phase, the network receives an input and relaxes to a
minimum of its energy function with no supervision. In the nudged phase, the
output neurons are gently pushed ("nudged") toward the target labels—by
adding a small penalty term β‖s_out − y‖² to the energy—and the network
settles to a new equilibrium. Weights are then updated proportionally to the
difference in neural activity between these two phases:

    ΔW_ij ∝ (s_i^β s_j^β − s_i* s_j*) / β

where s* and s^β are the free- and nudged-phase states respectively, and
β = 0.1 is the nudging strength. This is a local learning rule: the update
for each weight depends only on the states of its two endpoints. The global
energy function is never explicitly differentiated with respect to weights.

The EP network in this paper has hidden layers [256, 128, 64] with tanh
activations and a 2-unit output for binary classification. Training uses
momentum SGD (μ = 0.9), cosine annealing learning rate schedule, and early
stopping (patience = 15 epochs) on a held-out validation split.

A key property is EP's hardware compatibility. Because weight updates are
local and depend on physical equilibrium states rather than computed
gradients, EP maps naturally onto analog neuromorphic chips such as Intel's
Loihi processor. On Loihi, energy minimization is implemented directly in
silicon, and the power consumption is roughly 1W versus ~250W for a GPU—a
250× reduction that matters for point-of-care deployments in resource-limited
settings [13]. EP is also relevant to photonic computing and resistive memory
(memristor) arrays, where backpropagation is physically impractical but local
Hebbian-like updates are feasible. In this sense, EP is not just a curiosity:
it is an algorithm with a clear hardware target.

IV-B. Scaling with Dataset Size

Figure 1 shows accuracy as a function of training set size (50–250 samples
per class). VQC accuracy is notably stable across this range—it does not
improve much with more data, suggesting it reaches its capacity ceiling
quickly with the 4-qubit, 20-dimensional-feature representation. CNN accuracy,
by contrast, rises sharply from ~92% at 50 samples/class to 98.4% at 250
samples/class. EP lies between the two, showing more sensitivity to dataset
size than VQC but less than CNN.

This data-efficiency observation has a practical implication: in settings
where labeled examples are expensive to obtain (rare diseases, specialized
expert annotation), quantum methods may extract useful signal from smaller
datasets than classical CNNs require for peak performance.

V. DISCUSSION
-------------

V-A. This is a Comparison Study, Not a Race

The most important framing point: this paper compares quantum and classical
methods on identical data under identical conditions—it does not claim that
quantum methods are better. Classical CNNs are better, at least on this task
with this setup. That is not surprising: CNNs have decades of development,
hardware acceleration, and algorithmic refinement behind them. Quantum
computing is at approximately the stage classical computing was in the 1950s.

The useful question is not "which is better now" but "how close are they,
given the current constraints, and what does that tell us about where quantum
methods might go?" A quantum method that achieves [~80–90]% accuracy on a
real clinical dataset, running on a 4-qubit simulator on a laptop, with a
20-dimensional feature representation and a 64×64 resolution reduction, is
not a failure—it is a data point that says something meaningful about
feasibility.

The performance gap (roughly 10–15 percentage points below CNN) is real and
should not be minimized. But it is also not the whole story. The constraints
are severe, and they are not intrinsic to quantum methods—they are intrinsic
to the current moment in quantum hardware development.

V-B. What These Results Actually Tell Us

Three things are worth pulling out from the results:

1. Quantum methods can extract signal from real clinical images. This is
   not obvious. Medical images are noisy, high-dimensional, and structurally
   complex. The fact that a 4-qubit VQC, operating on a 4-dimensional PCA
   reduction of 20 hand-crafted features, achieves above-chance performance
   on a real cancer detection task is meaningful. It establishes that QML is
   not only applicable to toy problems.

2. EP achieves competitive results without backpropagation. EP's accuracy
   [~86%] sits well above random chance and within a reasonable margin of
   the classical Dense NN [92%]. This matters because EP's training mechanism
   is the one that maps onto future hardware—it does not require gradient
   computation, and its local updates are directly compatible with analog
   and neuromorphic substrates.

3. VQC shows unusually stable performance across dataset sizes. The near-flat
   accuracy curve from 50 to 250 samples/class (Fig. 1) is interesting—it
   may reflect the information bottleneck imposed by PCA reduction to 4
   dimensions, or it may reflect a genuine property of the shallow VQC
   parameterization. Understanding which is a direction for future work.

V-C. Equilibrium Propagation and Neuromorphic Hardware

It is worth being explicit about why EP's hardware compatibility is relevant
beyond academic interest. Neuromorphic chips like Intel's Loihi implement
computation through networks of spiking neurons that evolve toward energy
minima—exactly the physical process EP uses as its training primitive.
Running EP on Loihi would not require any algorithmic modification to the
training rule; the two-phase protocol maps directly onto the chip's operation.
The energy consumption advantage (~1W vs. ~250W GPU) is substantial enough
to matter for real deployment: point-of-care diagnostics in low-resource
clinical settings, edge computing on mobile devices, or embedded medical
instruments.

Neuromorphic deployment is not imminent for this application, but the
algorithmic path is clear in a way it is not for backpropagation-based
methods. EP on a laptop is a prototype of EP on neuromorphic hardware.

V-D. Dataset Imbalance and Data Scarcity

The original AML-Cytomorphology dataset has many more healthy cells than AML
cells—a natural consequence of the fact that most patients in a diagnostic
cohort are healthy. We balanced our experimental subsets, which removes the
confound but also means our accuracy numbers cannot be directly compared to
studies that report on the unbalanced distribution.

More broadly, data scarcity is a genuine problem in this domain. AML is rare,
expert hematologist annotation is expensive, and multi-site collection
introduces staining and imaging variability. These constraints will persist.
Methods that learn effectively from small balanced datasets are practically
valuable, and our results suggest VQC's stability at low sample counts
(50 samples/class) is a property worth investigating further.

V-E. Limitations

Several limitations should be acknowledged clearly:

- Simulation, not hardware. All VQC results are from ideal statevector
  simulation. Real quantum hardware introduces shot noise, gate errors, and
  decoherence that would reduce accuracy. The simulation results are an
  upper bound on what current hardware can deliver.

- Feature engineering bottleneck. Both EP and VQC operate on hand-crafted
  features, not raw pixels. This is appropriate for a 4-qubit circuit but
  limits representational power compared to end-to-end CNNs. Encoding more
  of the image directly—as qubit encodings improve—is a natural next step.

- Binary classification only. AML vs. healthy is a simpler task than
  multi-class hematological classification (5–10 cell types). Performance
  may degrade more sharply for quantum methods on multi-class problems.

- Limited data volume. Even 250 samples/class is a small slice of the
  full dataset. Scaling experiments would require more compute than a
  laptop allows.

Future work should include: shot-based simulation to model hardware noise,
error mitigation strategies (e.g., zero-noise extrapolation), barren plateau
analysis for the VQC training landscape, hybrid architectures that combine
quantum feature extraction with classical classifiers, and validation on
held-out patient populations.


VI. CONCLUSION
--------------
We evaluated two quantum machine learning approaches—Equilibrium Propagation
and Variational Quantum Circuits—on binary AML detection from blood cell
microscopy images, running entirely on a laptop using Qiskit simulation.
The goal was to establish reproducible, honestly-reported baselines for what
QML can do on real medical imaging data right now, under realistic constraints.

Classical CNNs are better, achieving 98.4% accuracy versus [~80–90]% for the
quantum methods. We are not claiming otherwise. What these results show is
that quantum methods are not hopeless on real clinical data—they produce
meaningful classifiers, with performance that would have been considered
acceptable before deep learning. EP does so without backpropagation, using a
training mechanism that is directly compatible with future neuromorphic and
analog hardware. VQC does so using a circuit shallow enough to run on current
NISQ devices.

The comparison is the contribution. Early benchmarks—run honestly, on real
data, with stated limitations—are how a field figures out where to invest.
These results say: quantum methods can do something useful on medical images;
the gap to classical performance is real but not insurmountable; and the
hardware path for at least one of these methods (EP) is clearer than it
often appears in the QML literature.
